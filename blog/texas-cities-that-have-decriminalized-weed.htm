Texas Cities That Have Decriminalized Weed

The landscape of cannabis laws in Texas is shifting, albeit slowly. While the state remains one of the most conservative when it comes to marijuana legalization, several cities have taken matters into their own hands by decriminalizing small amounts of weed. This local movement reflects a growing national trend toward cannabis reform, even in traditionally red states.

The Current State of Marijuana Laws in Texas

Texas still classifies marijuana as a controlled substance under state law. Possession of even small amounts can lead to arrests, fines, and jail time. However, in recent years, a handful of Texas cities have adopted policies to reduce penalties for minor cannabis offenses.

What Does Decriminalization Mean?

Decriminalization doesn’t mean marijuana is legal—it simply means that possessing small amounts (usually under one or two ounces) is treated as a low-priority offense, often resulting in a fine rather than jail time. This approach reduces the burden on law enforcement and keeps minor offenders out of the criminal justice system.

Texas Cities Leading the Charge

Several cities in Texas have passed ordinances to decriminalize marijuana at the local level. Here are some of the most notable:

Austin

Austin has long been a progressive outlier in Texas. In 2022, voters overwhelmingly approved Proposition A, which effectively decriminalized possession of small amounts of marijuana. Under this policy, Austin police no longer arrest or issue citations for possession of up to four ounces of cannabis unless it’s part of a high-priority investigation (such as felony distribution cases).

Dallas

Dallas hasn’t fully decriminalized weed, but it has taken steps to reduce penalties. The city’s "cite-and-release" program allows officers to issue citations instead of making arrests for minor marijuana possession. While this doesn’t eliminate penalties entirely, it helps prevent unnecessary jail time for non-violent offenders.

Houston

Harris County, which includes Houston, implemented a diversion program for first-time offenders caught with small amounts of marijuana. Instead of facing criminal charges, individuals can opt for a drug education course. This policy has significantly reduced marijuana-related arrests in the county.

San Antonio

Bexar County, home to San Antonio, has also adopted a cite-and-release policy for minor cannabis possession. However, enforcement varies, and some activists argue that more comprehensive decriminalization is needed.

Smaller Cities Joining the Movement

It’s not just major metros making changes. Cities like El Paso, Killeen, and Denton have also pushed for decriminalization through local ordinances or law enforcement discretion.

Why Are These Changes Happening Now?

Public Opinion Shifts

Nationwide, support for marijuana legalization is at an all-time high. Even in Texas, polls show that a majority of residents favor decriminalization or full legalization. This shift in public sentiment is putting pressure on local governments to act.

Racial and Social Justice Concerns

Marijuana criminalization has disproportionately impacted communities of color. Decriminalization efforts are often framed as a step toward correcting these injustices by reducing unnecessary arrests and convictions.

Economic and Law Enforcement Benefits

Prosecuting minor marijuana offenses consumes significant law enforcement resources. Decriminalization allows police to focus on more serious crimes while reducing jail overcrowding.

Challenges and Opposition

Despite progress, decriminalization efforts face pushback. Some law enforcement officials argue that relaxing marijuana laws could lead to increased use or public safety concerns. Additionally, state lawmakers have occasionally threatened to overturn local ordinances, creating legal uncertainty.

The State vs. Local Control Battle

Texas has a history of preempting local policies it disagrees with. While cities like Austin have successfully implemented decriminalization, state officials could still intervene, especially under conservative leadership.

What’s Next for Texas?

The trend toward decriminalization is likely to continue, especially as neighboring states like New Mexico and Oklahoma embrace more progressive cannabis laws. However, full legalization in Texas remains a distant prospect unless there’s a major shift in state politics.

For now, the cities that have taken steps toward decriminalization serve as test cases for how Texas might handle marijuana reform in the future. Whether this movement will spread statewide—or face resistance from lawmakers—remains to be seen.

As the national conversation around cannabis evolves, Texas finds itself at a crossroads. Will it follow the lead of its more progressive cities, or will state-level politics keep marijuana criminalized for years to come? Only time will tell.
