How to Draft Legal Opinions as an Intern

Drafting legal opinions is a critical skill for any law intern, especially in today’s fast-evolving legal landscape. Whether you’re working at a corporate firm, a public interest organization, or a government agency, the ability to analyze complex issues and present clear, well-reasoned arguments is invaluable. This guide will walk you through the essentials of drafting effective legal opinions while addressing contemporary global challenges like AI regulation, climate litigation, and digital privacy.

Understanding the Purpose of a Legal Opinion

Before diving into the drafting process, it’s crucial to understand what a legal opinion is and why it matters. A legal opinion is a formal document that analyzes a specific legal issue, applies relevant laws and precedents, and provides recommendations or conclusions. It’s often requested by clients, senior attorneys, or courts to clarify uncertainties or support decision-making.

Key Components of a Legal Opinion

  1. Issue Identification – Clearly define the legal question you’re addressing.
  2. Factual Background – Summarize the relevant facts without unnecessary details.
  3. Legal Analysis – Apply statutes, case law, and regulations to the facts.
  4. Conclusion – Provide a concise answer or recommendation.

Step-by-Step Guide to Drafting a Legal Opinion

1. Research Thoroughly

Start by gathering all relevant legal materials, including:
- Statutes and Regulations – Check federal, state, and international laws where applicable.
- Case Law – Look for precedents from courts in your jurisdiction.
- Secondary Sources – Law review articles, treatises, and policy papers can provide context.

Pro Tip: Use AI-powered legal research tools like Westlaw or LexisNexis to save time, but always verify primary sources.

2. Structure Your Opinion Logically

A well-organized opinion enhances readability and persuasiveness. Follow this structure:

Heading

  • Include the title (e.g., "Legal Opinion on Data Privacy Compliance Under GDPR").
  • Add the date and your name (or the firm’s name).

Introduction (Optional but Helpful)

Briefly state the purpose of the opinion and the scope of your analysis.

Statement of Facts

Present the key facts objectively. Avoid assumptions or editorializing.

Legal Analysis

Break this into sub-sections for clarity:
- Applicable Law – Summarize the relevant legal framework.
- Application to Facts – Explain how the law applies to the situation.
- Counterarguments – Address potential weaknesses in your reasoning.

Conclusion

Restate your findings in a clear, actionable manner.

3. Write with Clarity and Precision

Legal writing should be:
- Concise – Avoid legalese where plain language suffices.
- Structured – Use headings, bullet points, and numbered lists.
- Authoritative – Cite sources accurately and consistently.

Example: Instead of saying, "It could be argued that…," say, "Courts have held that… [cite case]."

4. Address Contemporary Legal Challenges

Modern legal opinions often involve cutting-edge issues. Here’s how to approach some of them:

AI and Algorithmic Accountability

With the rise of generative AI, legal opinions on liability for AI-generated content are in demand. Consider:
- Who is liable if an AI defames someone?
- How do copyright laws apply to AI-created works?

Climate Change Litigation

Firms are increasingly asked to assess risks related to environmental regulations. Key questions include:
- Can shareholders sue companies for failing to meet net-zero pledges?
- What are the legal implications of carbon offsetting schemes?

Digital Privacy and Cybersecurity

Data breaches and privacy violations are rampant. Focus on:
- Compliance with GDPR, CCPA, or other privacy laws.
- Legal remedies for victims of data leaks.

5. Review and Edit Meticulously

Before submitting your opinion:
- Check for Accuracy – Ensure all legal citations are correct.
- Eliminate Ambiguity – Rewrite vague or convoluted sentences.
- Get Feedback – Ask a senior attorney to review your draft.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Overgeneralizing – Tailor your analysis to the specific facts.
  • Ignoring Contrary Authority – Acknowledge and distinguish unfavorable precedents.
  • Poor Formatting – Use consistent fonts, margins, and citation styles.

Final Thoughts

Mastering legal opinion drafting takes practice, but by following these steps, you’ll develop a skill that sets you apart as an intern. Stay updated on global legal trends, refine your research techniques, and always prioritize clarity and precision in your writing.
